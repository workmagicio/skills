## Diff patterns (interpretation playbook)

When two models differ noticeably (> 15-20% delta), match against the patterns below and use the corresponding business-language explanation. **Do not invent reasons** — if no pattern matches, surface that fact instead of guessing.

| **Pattern** | **Business-language explanation** |
|-|-|
| `platform_reported >> last_click` | Two compounding sources of the gap: **(1) Cross-channel double-counting** — Meta, Google, and TikTok each take credit for the same conversion if all touched the user. WorkMagic last_click deduplicates by giving credit only to the platform that owned the last click. **(2) View-based attribution included** — platforms also credit view-through conversions (the user saw the ad and converted without clicking), which click-based models like last_click cannot capture by definition. Expect the platform-reported sum across channels to exceed both total orders and last_click totals. |
| `last_click > dda` | Last click concentrates 100% credit on the final touch; dda spreads credit across all clicks in the conversion path. A channel that often appears late in the journey (retargeting-heavy Meta, branded Google search) will show stronger in last_click than dda. |
| `last_click > idda` (most common) | The classic "platform vs incremental" gap. Last click awards credit for every conversion it touched last; iDDA further discounts conversions that would have happened anyway (calibrated by lift test). The gap reflects the channel's **real incremental contribution** being lower than its observed last-touch share. |
| `dda > idda` | dda is the pure mathematical distribution; iDDA layers lift-test calibration on top. If the channel's most recent lift test concluded with a low `LIFT_PCT`, iDDA will deflate its weight below the dda baseline. Check the lift-test calendar to confirm. |
| `first_click > last_click` | This channel plays a discovery/awareness role — it brings users into the journey, but they convert through a different channel later. Meta and TikTok (especially video / branded campaigns) commonly show stronger in first_click than last_click. |
| `idda > last_click` (rare) | Two possible causes: **(1) View-through attribution (VTA)** credited in iDDA but ignored by last_click — common for high-impression brand-style campaigns on Meta / TikTok / Pinterest / Snap where users see the ad and convert without clicking. **(2) Lift-test calibration finds the channel highly incremental on touches that last_click can't measure well** — common for upper-funnel TikTok, CTV, YouTube where the touchpoint drives behavior but the conversion click lands on a different channel. Either way, iDDA is pricing in business impact that pure click-based attribution misses. |
| `idda ≈ dda` | No active lift-test calibration affecting this channel — either no lift test has been run, or the most recent test concluded with a neutral lift. iDDA is essentially passing dda through. |
| `idda ≈ last_click` | The channel behaves like a true last-touch driver with high incrementality — most of what it shows in last_click survives the calibration. Common for lower-funnel performance channels with strong conversion intent. |
| `All models close` (within \~10%) | Straightforward conversion paths, minimal cross-channel overlap, no big VTA component. The channel does roughly what it looks like it does. Often seen for small-volume tactics or channels with simple buyer journeys. |
| `platform_reported >> idda` (extreme) | Two things stacked: cross-channel double-counting (platform vs last_click gap) AND incrementality discount (last_click vs idda gap). Common in iOS-era Meta or post-cookie-deprecation Google where platforms aggressively claim conversions they didn't drive. The iDDA number is the closer estimate of business impact. |
